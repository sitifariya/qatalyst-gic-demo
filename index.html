The question

A power grid has to survive its worst day. Batteries help, but only if you put them in the right places. There are 21 billion ways to choose 8 locations out of 118. Which computer should pick?
The test we set
The grid. A standard 118-substation test network used by power engineers worldwide, based on the real American Electric Power system in Ohio.

The stress. A 400 MW AI data centre plugs in, and then the worst weather day of 2022 arrives: Winter Storm Elliott, minus 9.6 degrees, heating demand surging 24 percent. On that day the network cannot safely carry everything people are asking of it.

The decision. Place 8 batteries, 100 MW each. Good placement keeps demand flowing. Bad placement wastes a billion-dollar investment.

The contest. Five different computer methods, including a D-Wave quantum hybrid, each given exactly 120 seconds. Same problem, same clock, same scoring. One of them can also prove its answer is the best possible, which gives us a yardstick almost no benchmark has.
Everything on this site is a measured result. Nothing is simulated for demonstration.

The budget test: the most realistic problem, and the clearest win

Real sites cost different amounts, and plans must fit a budget. We capped spending so the old favourite plan was unaffordable, for every method equally. The everyday greedy method broke. The quantum hybrid, unchanged, came first.

When this matters

Real planning is a stream of what-ifs. New budget, a refused site, a bigger data centre. And some changes don't wait for a study: a line goes down, or an EV charging hub suddenly pulls far more power than planned, and the plan must be redone now.

Our test showed what happens when just one new rule appears. A spending cap, and the standard method didn't error, it quietly returned plans with two batteries missing. A wrong answer that looks right, flowing straight into someone's decision.

We fixed it, but that took an engineer: time to notice, time to understand why it broke, time to rebuild and recheck. Hours or days, while the pipeline keeps running on wrong answers. For the broken line or the surging chargers, the fix arrives after the moment that needed it.

The hybrid took the new rule as one extra line, same right answer, every run, straight away. When the grid changes faster than a method can be re-tuned, the method that never needs re-tuning wins.

How we tested

Public data, identical conditions, and every mistake we made along the way written down.
The grid. The IEEE 118-bus test system, the standard research network, loaded through the open-source pandapower library. Real connections, real demand at each substation.

The weather. Real hourly temperatures for Columbus, Ohio in 2022, from the US National Renewable Energy Laboratory. We convert temperature into electricity demand using published sensitivities. The worst day in that record is Winter Storm Elliott.

The rules. Every method got exactly the same amount of time and was scored by exactly the same code. Methods that involve randomness were run several times and we report the range, not the best run. Cloud waiting time is counted, not hidden.

Where the certainty comes from. One method can prove its answer is the best possible. That proof is what lets us say how far every other method is from perfect, instead of just comparing them to each other.

The budget experiment. After the main campaign we added the most realistic variant: every substation has its own build cost (busier sites cost more) and the plan must fit a hard spending cap set so the old favourite plan is unaffordable. Every method faced the identical constraint. The everyday greedy method broke, spending its budget on expensive sites and finishing with only six of eight batteries. We then rebuilt greedy to shop by value for money, and that repaired version ties the hybrid. The hybrid itself needed no change at all: the budget was one extra line in its problem description.

Mistakes we found and corrected. An early result suggesting a 45% quantum win did not reproduce, so we withdrew it. One version of our scoring was physically wrong and was rebuilt. An early comparison gave methods unequal time, and was redone. We once described the hybrid's answer as the best known, and withdrew that when the exact solver proved otherwise.

Map geography. The test network has no real coordinates, so we placed it over Ohio for readability. The connections, the demand and every result are real.
